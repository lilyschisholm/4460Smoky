Recovering Resilience: The Great Smoky Mountains

How the Great Smoky Mountains recovered from air pollution, acidic streams, and renewed biodiversity.

By Team YALK: Yulin Lu, Chih-Ying Lee, Lily Chisholm, Katelyn Gumagay

• • • • • • •

The Hidden Story of Recovery

For decades, the Great Smoky Mountains suffered from the invisible damage of industrial pollution — murky air, acidified rain, and stressed wildlife. But over time, coordinated environmental policies, conservation efforts, and ecosystem resilience have led to signs of recovery.

This visualization tells that story through data. We represent factors of the park including:
park visibility, shown through the amount of particulate matter in the air, water quality, shown through ANC levels in the water, and biodiversity of the various types of species existing in the park. Each dataset reveals a layer of resilience within the Smokies’ ecosystem.

Clearing the Air

The park’s iconic views once symbolized beauty — until air pollution turned it gray. The graph below tracks visibility improvements over time, showing the results of decades of emission reductions after the Clean Air Act and many other conservation efforts made by the national park servies. These efforts can be shown by the visualization as the less particulate matter there is, the more visible the Smoky Mountains will be. This is compared to the park's visitation data, displaying the direct correlation between park visbility and visitation.

Note: PM2.5(µg/m³) refers to the concentration of fine particulate matter with a diameter of 2.5 micrometers or less, measured in micrograms per cubic meter of air.

Less air pollution means more visitors: Over time, visibility improves as emissions decline — a clear sign that air quality policy changes are working. This can be shown by the opacity of the area graph reducing as times go on, directly correlating with the increased park visitation. However, visibility, and park visitation alongside it, is slightly decreasing as conservation efforts have slowed.

Clearer skies are only the beginning. As pollution declines, the mountains begin to heal in less visible ways — starting with the chemistry of their streams and rivers.

Streams That Remember

Acid rain stripped minerals from the Smokies’ soils, lowering the pH of mountain streams and endangering fish and other aquatic populations. The Acid Neutralizing Capacity (ANC) dataset tracks how well streams can buffer against acidification. Higher ANC means greater chemical stability and healthier habitats, allowing varieties species to thrive.
Each cell represents ANC at a specific stream site and year (1993–2023). Darker = low buffering, brighter = high buffering (healthier).

The water heatmap chart and the ANC trendline visualization are linked! Highlight a timeframe on the heatmap to see the ANC trend for that timeframe.

Spatial and Temporal Patterns (Heatmap)

You may be wondering about Site 0174: why does that particular site have such a high ANC? There isn't enough data to understand the exact cause, but deducing from the environment of the smokies, we can think of a few potential reasons! For example, this particular site might have an abundance of limestone bedrock, which has a lot of carbonates and bicarbonates, making it well suited for neutralizing acids. It could also be that this site has a lot of decomposing organic matter, and thus releases a lot of carbon dioxide, which can help neutralize acids.

Park-Wide ANC Trend (1993–2023)

The trendline below shows how the park’s average stream ANC has changed over time — a direct measure of how air-quality improvements affect water chemistry.

Slow Recovery following Efforts: Starting in the early 2000s, ANC values gradually rise — reflecting fewer acidic inputs and the slow recovery of soil and stream chemistry. Cleaner air means healthier waters.

As the chemistry stabilizes, the ecosystem can finally start to breathe again. Fish, insects, and amphibians — once driven out by acidic waters — begin to return to their natural habitats.

Life Returns to the Park

The park's biodiversity data shows how different species populations have changed over time, reflecting the ecosystem's response to environmental improvements. With this, we can see how populations of different species have increased or decreased over time, as well as see overall trends in biodiversity. These reflect the environment of the smokies, as well as conservation efforts. For example, in the 1990's - 2000's, when conservation efforts increased, you can see an overall increase in biodiversity! Decreases after this point are attributed to a loss of conservation efforts together with a lack of effort in recording of species.

Species' Comeback: The visualization shows population trends across different species groups. Use the dropdown to focus on specific taxa groups, and the timeline brush to zoom into specific time periods. Click on any area in the chart to focus on a specific species. The overall trend of each species show increasing populations, showing that current conservation efforts are working.

People Return to the Mountains

As the Smokies healed, so did our connection to them. Cleaner air and recovering streams invited people back.
The steady rise in visitation marks the return of trust in the land’s ability to breathe again.

From air to water, life to people — each layer of recovery tells the same story: ecosystems are interconnected, and healing in one part ripples through the rest.

The Bigger Picture

The Smokies’ story is part of a broader pattern across U.S. national parks — where decades of conservation and regulation have slowly reversed environmental decline. However, recent issues have affected the trajectory of improvement for many of these beautiful parks. Conclusion: Interconnected Recovery

The Great Smoky Mountains’ recovery is not just a story of cleaner air or better water — it’s a reminder that ecosystems heal as systems. When air clears, streams recover. When water stabilizes, fish return. And when life thrives, people come back to celebrate it. This shows the importance of conservation efforts. The Great Smoky Mountains have benefited greatly from the efforts of the National Park Services, showing how far conservation effects can reach. We urge these efforts to be continued; the importance of these efforts effects every aspect of our local, national, and global ecosystems.

This cycle — from pollution to protection, from decline to renewal — shows that resilience is measurable, visualizable, and incredibly important.
